What is Network Jitter and How to Optimize LAN Latency for Gaming

High ping variance and packet arrival delays ruin online gaming. Learn what network jitter is, how to measure ping stability, and how to optimize your LAN connection.

In online multiplayer gaming and VoIP video calls, consistency is everything. You might have a 500 Mbps connection, but if your games are stuttering, "network jitter" is likely the culprit. Jitter represents the variation in latency over time, and reducing it is crucial for a responsive internet connection.

1. Decoupling Latency (Ping) from Jitter

Latency (ping) is the total time it takes for a packet of data to travel from your computer to a server and back. Jitter is the variance in that ping. For example, if your ping fluctuates constantly between 20ms and 120ms, your jitter is high, which causes sudden lag spikes, rubber-banding, and disconnected sessions.

2. Why Wi-Fi is Prone to High Jitter

Wireless connections suffer from radio frequency interference, wall obstacles, and channel sharing. Even if your average ping on Wi-Fi is 30ms, packet drops and retries will cause jitter spikes. Switching to a wired Ethernet cable (Cat5e or Cat6) provides a dedicated path, dropping jitter below 1ms.

3. Optimize Network Adapter Settings

Open Device Manager on Windows, find your Ethernet Network Adapter, right-click and go to Properties -> Advanced. Disable features like "Energy Efficient Ethernet" and "Green Ethernet" to prevent the adapter from entering power-saving states, which introduces latency variance.

❓ Quick FAQs
What is a good jitter score for gaming?

A jitter score below 1ms to 2ms is considered excellent and represents a highly stable wired connection. Jitter above 15ms will result in noticeable lag spikes in online games.
